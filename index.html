The Physics Behind the Simulation

In a stable circular orbit, Earth's velocity is precisely balanced with the Sun's gravitational pull. The orbital velocity for a circular orbit is given by:

vcircular = √(GM/r)

The escape velocity—the minimum speed needed to break free from gravity—is:

vescape = √(2GM/r) = √2 × vcircular

This √2 factor is the key to everything. Earth's circular orbit velocity is exactly 1/√2 ≈ 70.7% of escape velocity.

The 50% Threshold

When we suddenly remove mass from the Sun, Earth's velocity stays the same but the escape velocity changes:

vescape,new = √(2G × Mnew/r)

At exactly 50% solar mass, something magical happens. The new escape velocity becomes:

vescape,new = √(2G × 0.5M/r) = √(GM/r) = vcircular

Earth's existing velocity exactly equals the new escape velocity! This produces a parabolic trajectory—the boundary between bound and unbound orbits.

Three Possible Fates

> 50% Mass Remains
Elliptical Orbit (Bound)

Total energy E < 0. Earth swings out to a distant aphelion but returns. The less mass removed, the smaller the ellipse.

= 50% Mass Remains
Parabolic Escape (E = 0)

Earth escapes to infinity, velocity approaching zero asymptotically. The mathematical knife-edge between bound and unbound.

< 50% Mass Remains
Hyperbolic Escape (Unbound)

Total energy E > 0. Earth escapes with residual velocity—it will still be moving even at infinite distance.

The Numerical Precision Problem

Here's where physics meets computer science. In this simulation, you might notice that 50% sometimes shows as "Hyperbolic" or "Elliptical" instead of "Parabolic." This isn't a bug—it's a fundamental limitation of floating-point arithmetic.

Computers represent numbers in binary with finite precision. When we calculate 50%, we might actually get 50.00000000001% or 49.99999999999%. The difference between these is physically meaningful:

  • 50.00000000001% → E slightly positive → Hyperbolic (escapes with tiny residual velocity)
  • 50.00000000000% → E = 0 exactly → Parabolic (velocity → 0 at infinity)
  • 49.99999999999% → E slightly negative → Elliptical (returns after millions of years)

This is why real astrophysics simulations use double precision (64-bit) floating-point numbers, which provide about 16 significant digits. Even then, for problems sensitive to exact thresholds, mathematicians prefer pencil-and-paper solutions with symbolic math.

The elegance of this problem is that the answer involves √2—an irrational number that cannot be represented exactly in any finite decimal or binary expansion. The 50% threshold is mathematically perfect but numerically unreachable.

Light-Speed Gravity

This simulation includes one more realistic detail: gravitational changes propagate at the speed of light, not instantaneously. When the Sun's mass changes, Earth continues on its original orbit for ~8.3 minutes until the gravitational "news" arrives. This is consistent with General Relativity, where gravitational waves travel at c.

Time to Escape

For the parabolic case (50% mass), the time to reach distance r from an initial circular orbit at r₀ is:

t = (1/3)√(4/GM₀) × (r3/2 - r₀3/2)

For Earth starting at 1 AU with half the Sun's mass remaining:

  • Reach 10 AU: ~2.5 years
  • Reach 100 AU: ~80 years
  • Reach 1000 AU: ~2,500 years
  • Reach infinity: ∞ (but velocity → 0)

This simulation was created to explore a classic orbital mechanics problem: what happens to a planet's orbit when its star suddenly loses mass? The answer reveals beautiful connections between energy conservation, the mathematics of conic sections, and the practical limits of numerical computation.
