The Physics Behind the Simulation

In a stable circular orbit, Earth's velocity is precisely balanced with the Sun's gravitational pull. The orbital velocity for a circular orbit is given by:

vcircular = √(GM/r)

The escape velocity, the minimum speed needed to break free from gravity, is:

vescape = √(2GM/r) = √2 × vcircular

This √2 factor is the key to everything. Earth's circular orbit velocity is exactly 1/√2 ≈ 70.7% of escape velocity.

The 50% Threshold

When we suddenly remove mass from the Sun, Earth's velocity stays the same but the escape velocity changes:

vescape,new = √(2G × Mnew/r)

At exactly 50% solar mass, the new escape velocity becomes:

vescape,new = √(2G × 0.5M/r) = √(GM/r) = vcircular

Earth's existing velocity exactly equals the new escape velocity. This produces a parabolic trajectory, the boundary between bound and unbound orbits.

Three Possible Fates

> 50% Mass Remains
Elliptical Orbit (Bound)

Total energy E < 0. Earth swings out to a distant aphelion but returns. The less mass removed, the larger the ellipse.

= 50% Mass Remains
Parabolic Escape (E = 0)

Earth escapes to infinity, velocity approaching zero asymptotically. The mathematical knife edge between bound and unbound.

< 50% Mass Remains
Hyperbolic Escape (Unbound)

Total energy E > 0. Earth escapes with residual velocity, it will still be moving even at infinite distance.

The Numerical Precision Problem

You might notice that 50% sometimes shows as Hyperbolic or Elliptical instead of Parabolic. This is a limitation of floating point arithmetic.

Computers represent numbers in binary with finite precision. When we calculate 50%, we might actually get 50.00000000001% or 49.99999999999%. The difference between these is physically meaningful:

  • 50.00000000001% → E slightly positive → Hyperbolic
  • 50.00000000000% → E = 0 exactly → Parabolic
  • 49.99999999999% → E slightly negative → Elliptical

Light Speed Gravity

This simulation includes one more realistic detail: gravitational changes propagate at the speed of light, not instantaneously. When the Sun's mass changes, Earth continues on its original orbit for about 8.3 minutes until the gravitational news arrives.

This simulation explores a classic orbital mechanics problem: what happens to a planet's orbit when its star suddenly loses mass? The answer connects energy conservation, conic sections, and the practical limits of numerical computation.
